Hamsters

In 1839,

The Syrian hamster, also known as the "golden" hamster, is the best known species of hamster, commonly kept as a pet. It was scientifically discovered by George Robert Waterhouse. Researchers were not able to successfully breed and domesticate hamsters until 1939.

Whiskers, Wiggles & Wonder

Hamsters are small, nocturnal rodents that are popular as pets, known for their playful nature and unique characteristics. Hamsters are small, compact animals with short tails, furry ears, and sturdy legs. Their thick, silky fur varies in length and comes in colors such as black, grey, white, brown, and honey, depending on the species.

Senses

Hamsters have poor eyesight and are colorblind, but they are still curious and love to explore.
They rely on their strong sense of smell and hearing to find food, recognize their babies, and sense nearby movement.
Scent glands help them mark territory, and they can even learn to recognize their owner's voice!

Basic Care For Your Hamster

Germany is known for their high standards of hamster care. The guide below are some essentials to ensuring a pet hamster has the basic care it needs in setting up its enclosure!


Cage Size: The Bundesministerium für Ernährung und Landwirtschaft recommends a minimum of 5,000 square centimeters for a hamster cage.


Burrowing: The recommended minimum height for bedding is 20 centimeters, the more the better!


Substrate: Any paper-based bedding, hemp shavings, aspen shavings, birch shavings and spruce shavings.


Wheel: A necessity for every hamster! Silent runners, silent spinners, rodent wheel, NiteAngel wheels and cork and wooden wheels are safe options.



Hamster Enrichment

Forage: Sprinkle your hamster's main diet, safe herbs, leaves, sprays and flowers all over the enclosure. This allows for your hamster to look for it, with it's extremely good sense of smell, it also helps to stimulate their senses with a range of new smells.


Different Substrates: Provide your hamster with multiple different substrates, it allows your hamster another texture to touch and dig in rather than burrow in. A sand bath helps remove any excess oils from its coat and also gives them an enrichment for digging as well as it can help keep claw worn down.


Hideouts: Place multiple hideouts for them to choose from, allowing your hamster to quickly go to another hideout to feel safe as they tend to not like to be in open space too much.

Food hoarding: Hamsters carry food in their spacious cheek pouches and bring it to their underground chambers to store it for later. When their cheeks are full, it can make their heads double or triple in size!

Burrowing behaviour: Hamsters are skilled diggers, creating burrows with multiple entrances and connected chambers for nesting, food storage, and shelter. They dig using their legs, snouts, and teeth. In the wild, these burrows help regulate temperature and provide protection from predators.

Social behaviour: Most hamsters are strictly solitary and may become highly stressed or aggressive when housed together, sometimes resulting in serious fights. Hamsters communicate through body language and scent marking, using scent glands to convey information and express their mood.

Sleep-wake cycles: Hamsters are often described as nocturnal or crepuscular, meaning they are most active at dawn and dusk. While some sources label them as nocturnal, others suggest their natural behavior is primarily crepuscular, as they typically emerge briefly before sunset and return to their burrows after dark.


Diet

A balanced diet is vital for a hamster's health. They need protein, fats, carbohydrates, vitamins, and minerals, with at least 18% protein recommended. Pellets or lab blocks are ideal to prevent selective feeding. Small amounts of vegetables like carrots, broccoli, and spinach can be added. Fruits and herbs may be offered occasionally.

Treats: such as seeds, nuts, dried fruits, and mealworms should be limited.
Supplements like vitamin C, calcium blocks, or a little plain yogurt may support overall health.

Remember:

  1. Introduce new foods gradually

  2. Limit treats

  3. Always provide fresh water
